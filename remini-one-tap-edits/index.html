Remini — One-Tap Edits

How a fifteen-year-old AI photo app explored conversational editing, found that the chat wasn't the answer, and reshaped the interaction model into something faster, simpler, and three times more effective.

Year
2026 — Ongoing
Role
Product Designer · Acting PM on AI Edits
Tools
Figma, FigJam, Cursor, Notion, BigQuery

The challenge

Remini built its name on one thing: a button that fixed your photo. Tap, wait, done. That simplicity carried the product to over 100 million downloads — but a decade in, the AI photo space had moved on. New competitors were shipping conversational editors where users could describe what they wanted in natural language, and the question for Remini wasn't whether to follow, but how.

The brief I started from was open: bring Remini into the conversational AI era. The first hypothesis was the obvious one — let users talk to their photos. Type "make it brighter," "remove the background," "give me a 90s look," and the AI does it. We built it. We shipped it as Textual Edits. And then we ran the experiment.

The first try: Textual Edits

The first version of Textual Edits put the prompt at the center. A user uploaded a photo, opened the editor, and saw an empty input box waiting for them to describe their edit. Below it, a row of suggested prompts to nudge them in. Above it, the photo. Pure conversational interaction, with the suggestions as a soft fallback.

We A/B-tested it against the existing AI Edits flow on 800,000+ new users between December 2025 and April 2026. The result was both surprising and clarifying: on the metrics that mattered to the business, the two flows were essentially indistinguishable. Trial rate was the same (~12.7% in both arms). Paid conversion was the same (~3% in both arms). Revenue per user was the same. The chat experience wasn't winning, but it wasn't losing either.

The interesting signal was inside the funnel. Textual Edits had a higher save completion rate than AI Edits — but the way users got there told a different story. A large share of the prompts users typed were vague, off-brand, or produced results they didn't want to keep. The chat had given them total freedom, and total freedom turned out to be the friction.

The insight

It took looking at the numbers next to the qualitative behavior to see it. Users didn't want a blank canvas. They wanted to be guided into making something good — fast. The chat had reframed the editor as a writing exercise; what users actually wanted was a starting point.

The conclusion shaped the next iteration: the value of an AI editor isn't the input method. It's how quickly the user gets to a result they're proud of.

The pivot: One-Tap Edits

For the new flow, I redesigned AI Edits around a single principle: one tap to a starting point, one line to refine it. The empty prompt was gone. The new entry point led with a curated set of presets — pre-trained, brand-safe, optimized for the kinds of edits Remini users actually save. The prompt input stayed, but as a second move: a refinement, not a starting state. One line, expandable, never required.

The interaction reads in a single glance: pick a preset, see the result, optionally refine in a line, save. Nothing in the way.

Early outcome

The new flow has been live as an experiment since late April 2026, with 200K+ users in the test cells. Five days into the rollout, the first-pass numbers are strong:

  • ~3x increase in edit completion vs. the previous AI Edits flow
  • Trial-to-paid conversion held steady at the same rate as the control
  • Engagement with the refinement line is meaningful but secondary — most users save after the preset, exactly as the design predicted

These are early-stage numbers and the experiment is still iterating. But the direction is clear enough that the team is already designing follow-up tests around the One-Tap model — extending it to other edit surfaces and exploring how far the "guided plus refinable" pattern can go.

What I learned

The instinct to chase the new interaction paradigm — chat, in this case — is strong, especially when competitors are doing it. But the lesson from Textual Edits was that the right paradigm depends on what the user is actually trying to do. Remini users aren't typing. They're scrolling, tapping, picking. The interaction model has to meet that reality, not fight it.

I learned to trust funnels more than features, and qualitative feedback more than analogies to other products. And I learned that in AI design, the hardest design decision is often how much agency to give the user — too little feels patronizing, too much feels like work. The space between is where the best AI products live.
