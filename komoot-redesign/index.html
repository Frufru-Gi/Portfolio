Komoot Redesign

A full product redesign of one of Europe's most-loved outdoor apps — across iOS, Android and Web — built during the most delicate moment in Komoot's history: the handover to a new owner, a new team, and a new chapter.

Year
2025
Role
Main Product Designer
Scope
iOS, Android, and Web
Tools
Figma, FigJam, Notion

The challenge

In early 2025, Bending Spoons acquired Komoot — a company with a fifteen-year history, a community of more than 50 million outdoor enthusiasts, and a product that people genuinely loved. I was the designer assigned to follow the acquisition, the handover from the original Komoot team, and the redesign that would mark the first major visible change of the new chapter.

This wasn't a routine rebrand. It was a moment of real transition — for the product, for the team that was passing it on, and for the community who had built habits around the app for years. The brief was clear in its ambition and unforgiving in its constraints: the new design had to feel modern and intuitive, but still feel like Komoot. The community had to recognize itself in the result.

The work was originally scoped as a one-month effort focused on a few key surfaces. Once we got into the codebase and the actual user flows, the truth was harder to ignore: the app had years of accumulated patches, an outdated visual language, and usability debt that couldn't be solved at the surface level. One month became three for the first rollout — then another three for additional screens and QA.

How I approached it

I worked as the main product designer, leading the design end to end across iOS, Android, and Web, with strategic guidance from a senior designer and, in the second half of the project, a second designer who joined to cover additional screens.

The first job was mapping the existing product in full. Before redesigning anything, I went through every screen of the live app — iOS, Android, and Web — and reconstructed the complete surface area: every flow, every state, every screen we had inherited. That mapping became the shared reference for the rest of the project: it told us what was actually there (often more than the previous documentation suggested), where the redundancies and inconsistencies lived, and which surfaces carried the most product weight.

From there, the work split naturally into three streams that ran in parallel: the foundations, the high-impact surfaces, and the long tail of details that decide whether a redesign feels finished or rushed.

The next job was understanding what Komoot actually was. Not what it looked like, not what the previous design language said it was — but what people came to it for, what they did with it, what they hated about it. We ran the work hand-in-hand with the community: 110 in-person interviews and over 3,000 online surveys to map preferences, friction points, and the elements people would have refused to lose. That research became the spine of every decision that followed.

From there, the priorities sharpened. We focused on the surfaces that carried the most product weight — onboarding, route discovery, the route planner, and the navigation experience — and rebuilt them around two principles: clarity over decoration, and continuity over reinvention. The design system got refreshed at the foundations level (color palette, typography, iconography, illustrations), but the navigation patterns and core workflows were preserved on purpose. People needed to recognize their app on the morning after the launch.

The harder calls were the quiet ones. Knowing what to leave alone. Knowing when "outdated" was actually "loved". Knowing that a community of 50 million people had earned the right to a redesign that didn't punish their muscle memory.

What made it hard

The technical surface was its own challenge — the app was old enough that some screens were carrying assumptions from a different era of mobile design, and some of those assumptions were bolted into the codebase. Every decision had to clear two bars: did it improve the experience, and could engineering ship it without rewriting more than the redesign was worth?

But the heaviest part of the project wasn't technical. It was the human one. Following an acquisition means working alongside people who built the thing you're now reshaping — people who care about it deeply, and are watching it pass into different hands. The original Komoot team had given the product fifteen years; my job was to honor that work while taking the product forward. There's no playbook for that kind of in-between moment, and a lot of the project was spent learning how to hold both things at once: respect for what was, and clarity about what needed to change.

The other thing was scope. The "one month, then we ship" plan didn't survive contact with the codebase. The rollout extended to six months total, in two phases, because the only thing worse than a slow redesign would have been a fast one that broke the habits of 50 million people.

Outcome

The new design launched on Web globally in September 2025, with Mobile following soon after. The community response, reported in Komoot's own newsroom, set a high bar:

  • Over 80% of users said they preferred the new design over the previous one
  • Another 15% said they liked it equally — meaning 95% of the community responded positively or neutrally to the change
  • Komoot's review and feedback channels remained healthy through the rollout

Beyond the launch numbers, the redesign became the first piece of an ambitious product roadmap. Within five months of the acquisition, the team had shipped over 50 changes to Web, Mobile, and connected devices. The redesigned foundations made that velocity possible: a cleaner system, a faster handover loop, and a design language that the team can keep iterating on without going back to first principles each time.

What I learned

This was the project that taught me design isn't only about the artifact. It's about the people who built what came before, the people who use what you're shipping, and the responsibility you carry as the bridge between the two.

I learned to slow down on the parts that looked easy and to speed up on the parts that looked impossible. I learned that respecting a product's history is a design skill, not a compromise. And I learned that the hardest decisions in a redesign aren't about what to change — they're about what to leave alone, even when leaving things alone is unfashionable.

If I did it again, I'd ask for more time at the very beginning to listen — to the previous team, to the community, to the codebase. The pressure of a one-month brief made us rush past the part of the work that mattered most.
